Every Latin letter you read traces back to a wall. The capital letters of our alphabet were perfected not by printers but by the carvers of Imperial Rome, whose inscriptions — most famously the base of **Trajan’s Column**, cut around 113 AD — set proportions so resolved that two thousand years of typographers have simply tried to live up to them. Cinzel is a faithful descendant of those letters, drawn for the screen by the Portuguese designer **Natanael Gama** and released, free and open source, in 2012.

The forms are pure Roman *capitalis monumentalis*. There is no lowercase, because the model has none: this is the alphabet as the Romans built it, in majuscules alone. The strokes swell and taper as a flat brush would have laid them out before the chisel followed — the technique, known as *brush-then-cut*, is why these serifs flare rather than sit flat, and why the whole face feels carved rather than printed.

What Gama added was range. The variable version runs from a quiet Regular to a monumental Black, so the same classical skeleton can whisper a wedding monogram or shout a film title. That breadth, plus the gravity the forms carry, is why Cinzel turns up wherever a brand wants to borrow a little permanence — luxury packaging, law firms, heritage logos, the opening titles of historical epics.

On this site it serves a second purpose. Cinzel is the **first chapter in the history of type** — the inscriptional root from which every serif, and ultimately every letter here, eventually grows.

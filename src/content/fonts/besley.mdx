The Industrial Revolution broke typography open. For three centuries type had been designed for one thing — the book — and at one rough size. Then came the poster, the handbill, the railway timetable and the advertisement, and printers suddenly needed letters that could be enormous, black, and legible from across a street. The genteel book faces were useless for it. The answer was the **slab serif**: take a sturdy letter and bolt heavy, rectangular serifs onto it. The Victorians, giddy with archaeology, called the loudest of them *Egyptians*.

The most enduring refinement of the style was **Clarendon**, cut in 1845 by Robert Besley at the Fann Street Foundry — a slab with *bracketed* serifs that curved gently into the stems, making it sturdy without being brutal. It was so successful that Besley took out one of the first-ever type patents on it. This typeface borrows his name in tribute.

Besley the typeface is, like Bodoni Moda, the work of **Owen Earl’s** open-source foundry *Indestructible Type* — a modern, variable Clarendon with a friendly warmth in its round ball terminals and a weight axis that climbs from a readable Regular to a poster-stopping Black. It keeps the low contrast and even colour that made the original work as ink at any size, while adding a genuine italic and the polish of a contemporary cut.

In the story of type it marks the turn outward — the moment letters stopped being only for reading and started being for *selling*, for shouting, for grabbing a passer-by. Every display face and every bold headline that follows owes something to the industrial slab.

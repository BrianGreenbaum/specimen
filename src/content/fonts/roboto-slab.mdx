When the Industrial Revolution needed letters that could shout from a wall, it bolted heavy rectangular **serifs** onto the roman and turned the volume up. The result — the **slab serif**, nicknamed *Egyptian* by Victorians swept up in the era’s Egyptomania — was the first typography designed for selling rather than reading: black, blunt and impossible to ignore from across a busy street.

Roboto Slab is that industrial muscle, brought to a modern, screen-ready calm. Drawn by **Christian Robertson** as the slab-serif sibling of the Roboto family, it keeps the block feet and low **contrast** that define the genre, but trades the Victorian rowdiness for the even, drafted skeleton of a contemporary sans. The effect is sturdy without being brutal — a letter that feels engineered.

The variable file runs from a hairline Thin to a heavy Black, so it can whisper a caption or anchor a headline with equal conviction. In the timeline it carries the DNA of the 19th-century poster straight into the interface age: proof that the slab, born to be huge and seen, still knows how to hold a screen.

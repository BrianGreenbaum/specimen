In the 1920s, a generation of designers decided the alphabet should be redesigned from first principles. The hand had no place in it; calligraphy was a relic. At the Bauhaus and in the circle around *Die Neue Typographie*, the new letter was to be **constructed** — assembled from the elementary shapes of geometry, the circle and the straight line, as rationally as a building. The masterpiece of the movement was **Futura**, drawn by Paul Renner in 1927: an o that is almost a true circle, an a reduced to a single storey, a face that still reads as the very sound of *modern*.

Jost is a love letter to that moment, built for the open web. It is — like Bodoni Moda and Besley — a project of **Owen Earl’s** *Indestructible Type*, and it captures Futura’s geometry with sharp apexes, compass-drawn bowls and a near-monoline evenness, while quietly fixing the things that make a purely geometric face hard to read at length.

The variable file gives it a vast range, from a whisper-thin Hairline to a solid Black, with a true italic alongside — enough to carry a whole identity, from delicate captions to a confident logotype. Set it in all caps, lightly tracked, and the 1920s come straight back.

In the timeline it is the great rationalist turn in the sans-serif: where the grotesque had been drawn by eye and feel, the geometric sans was *designed*, on a drafting table, from ideas. It is modernism made legible — and the direct ancestor of a century of clean, confident, circle-and-line brand type.

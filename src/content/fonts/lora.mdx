When **Olga Karpushina** and **Alexei Vanyashin** released Lora in 2011 through their Cyreal foundry, they set out to answer a question that preoccupied type designers in the early smartphone era: what does a serif look like when it is drawn for the screen from the start, rather than adapted from a form that was always meant to be pressed into paper? Their answer was a face that looks, at first glance, entirely classical — bracketed serifs, a moderate x-height, a harmonious rhythm — but that carries in its curves the memory of a brush, not a burin.

The calligraphic influence is subtle but consistent. Look closely at the terminals of the *a*, the *c*, and the *f*, and you find forms that lift off the stroke the way ink lifts off a nib: not sharp and mechanical, but slightly softened, as if the letter has just been written and the hand has barely left the page. This is Lora's signature warmth. It places it in a different lineage from the cold rationalism of Baskerville or the austerity of a Dutch transitional. It is closer in spirit to the Italian humanists — to a letter that was drawn, not engineered.

What sets Lora apart as a working screen font is the way those brushed qualities survive compression. Where a more delicate calligraphic face might lose its personality in a fourteen-pixel body, Lora's serifs are drawn assertively enough — thick brackets, strong baselines — that the texture stays alive at small sizes on a backlit display. It was built to be read for thousands of words at a stretch, in a browser or an e-reader or a CMS, and it performs that task with a composure that seems to come naturally rather than from effort.

Since its release it has become one of the most widely used serifs on the web — a quiet constant on editorial sites, literary blogs, and digital magazines around the world. It carries Cyrillic with the same authority as Latin, making it a practical choice for multilingual publishing. But its greatest distinction is simpler: it is a face that invites reading without announcing itself, whose warmth you feel before you can name it.

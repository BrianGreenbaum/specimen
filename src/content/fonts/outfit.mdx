The geometric sans was born on a drafting table. In the 1920s, **Paul Renner**’s Futura rebuilt the letter from the circle, the triangle and the line — a rejection of the calligraphic hand so complete that the alphabet became, in effect, a set of drawn ideas. Crisp and optimistic, it became the voice of the twentieth century and the ancestor of a hundred years of clean brand type.

Outfit is that geometry, dressed for the present. Drawn by **Rodrigo Fuenzalida** and developed as an open, brand-ready family, it keeps Futura’s circular bowls, single-storey a and **monoline** evenness, but rounds the corners of the idea: the forms are warmer, the widths steadier, the whole thing tuned to sit comfortably in a product interface or a logotype. Where Futura can feel austere, Outfit feels approachable.

Its even widths make it unusually tidy in motion and at small sizes, and the variable file spans a whisper-thin Thin to a confident Black. In the timeline it is the geometric school’s long afterglow — proof that the Bauhaus dream of a letter built from pure form never really ended; it just learned to smile.

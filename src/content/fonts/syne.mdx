**Syne** was not drawn for a brand guideline or an app. It was commissioned by **Synesthésie**, a contemporary-art centre outside Paris, and designed by the French studio **Bonjour Monde** as the voice of an institution that lives among artworks. That origin explains everything about it. Type made to share a wall with contemporary art cannot be quiet and well-mannered; it has to hold its own.

So Syne is, on purpose, a family that does not match. Across its styles the proportions lurch — a wide, splayed roman; an absurdly heavy *Extra* that clots into near-black; a tactile, almost engraved cut; a mono. Designers are invited to mix them, to let an enormous bold sit beside a spindly light in the same line and enjoy the friction. Where a corporate sans strives for one seamless system, Syne offers a set of mismatched siblings and dares you to introduce them.

The variable weight axis condenses part of that idea into a single slider: drive it upward and the letters thicken from an upright, slightly eccentric regular into a dense display black, counters squeezing shut as the ink takes over. It is a typeface that rewards being set large and loud — exhibition titles, record sleeves, posters that need to argue with the room.

Released free and open source, Syne brought a distinctly art-world sensibility to a web more used to neutral grotesques: proof that an open typeface can have a strong, strange point of view, and be more useful for it.

Most grotesques aim for a kind of invisible neutrality — the cleaner the better, every letter sanded to the same finish. **Bricolage Grotesque**, drawn by **Mathieu Triay** and released openly in 2022, goes the other way. Its name is the French word for *bricolage* — making something out of whatever happens to be at hand — and the typeface wears that spirit on its sleeve. The bones are a sturdy neo-grotesque, but the details are knowingly mismatched: a flicked leg here, an open ear there, terminals that don't all agree with one another.

The effect is a face with a pulse. Set a paragraph and it holds its colour like any good workhorse sans, helped by a tall x-height and even spacing; set a headline and the quirks step forward, giving the words a handmade warmth that polished grotesques sweat to avoid. It is the difference between a wall painted by a machine and one painted by a person — the small irregularities are the point.

Underneath the character sits real range. Bricolage is fully variable across **weight**, **width** and **optical size**, so a single file carries everything from a hairline caption to a compressed, heavy banner, adjusting its proportions as it grows. That makes it equally happy as interface type and as expressive display — a rare combination, and the reason it spread so quickly through editorial sites and design systems looking to sound a little more human.

Free and open source, Bricolage has become a kind of signature for a certain post-minimal moment on the web: confident, a touch rough, and entirely uninterested in pretending to be neutral.

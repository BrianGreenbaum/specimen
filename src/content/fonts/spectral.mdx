The old-style serif — warm, even, drawn from the broad-nib pen of the Renaissance scribes — has been the natural shape of the reading page for five hundred years. The challenge of the screen was to keep that warmth alive at small sizes, on backlit glass, where delicate strokes can vanish and tight counters fill with light.

Spectral, drawn by **Florian Runge** at **Production Type** and commissioned by Google, is one of the most considered answers. It carries the humanist skeleton — the **slanted stress**, the double-storey **g**, the smoothly **bracketed serifs** — but tunes every proportion for legibility on screen: a generous x-height, open counters, and contrast judged to survive rendering. It was conceived, fittingly, as a default serif for Google Docs and the web, where it is read more often than almost any face alive.

Across its weights and true italics it stays quiet and even, the colour of a well-set book. That is the highest compliment a text face can earn: set a long passage in Spectral and you stop noticing the type at all, which is exactly what it was built to do. In the timeline it is the old-style tradition, brought intact into the age of the screen.

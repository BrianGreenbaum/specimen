Some typefaces want to be looked at. Newsreader wants to be read *through* — to vanish the moment the reading begins, leaving only the argument on the page. Drawn by **Production Type** and released into the open in 2019, it belongs to the same rational lineage that **John Baskerville** opened in the 1750s: a serif drawn with reason and a ruler, its stress straightened toward the vertical, its contrast lifted just enough to feel sharp without ever flickering.

What makes it a creature of *this* century is the **optical-size axis**. Where Baskerville had to cut a separate punch for every size, Newsreader carries the whole range in one variable file: set it at caption size and the serifs thicken, the spacing breathes, the letters brace for legibility; push it to a headline and everything sharpens and tightens toward display elegance. It is the Enlightenment idea — one rational system, scaled by reason — finally made fluid.

It is, above all, an editorial workhorse: a face for journalism, essays and books on screen, with a true italic that leans in to quote and emphasise. In the timeline it stands where the serif turned from the pen toward the machine — and proves that the transitional temper, calm and literate, still reads as authority today.

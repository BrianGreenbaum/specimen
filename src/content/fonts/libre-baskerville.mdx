In the 1750s, in Birmingham, **John Baskerville** set out to print the most beautiful books he could. He smoothed his paper, blackened his ink and cut letters of crisp, high contrast and rational, upright stress — a clean break from the old-style faces before him that would come to define the *transitional* serif. Two and a half centuries later, his name still signals the printed page.

Libre Baskerville is the descendant built for the page Baskerville never saw: the screen. **Pablo Impallari** and **Rodrigo Fuenzalida**, working as Impallari Type out of Chile, took the American Type Founders’ 1941 cut of Baskerville as their starting point and asked what would have to change for it to read comfortably as body text at sixteen pixels in a web browser.

The answer was a careful list of compromises, each one trading a little of the original’s refinement for durability. The x-height was raised, so more of every word sits in the band the eye actually reads. The counters were widened, so letters do not clog up at small sizes. And the contrast was eased back, so the delicate hairlines that look so fine in metal do not flicker and disappear on a glowing display. Released in 2012 in just three styles — regular, italic and bold — it was never meant to be flashy. It was meant to get out of the way.

It succeeded so well that it became one of the quiet staples of the open web: the serif you reach for when an article, an essay or a long quotation simply needs to be read, at length, without fuss — Baskerville’s two-hundred-and-fifty-year-old ambition, finally legible in a browser tab.

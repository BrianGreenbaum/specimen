In 1592 a Frankfurt foundry run by the heirs of Egenolff, managed by **Conrad Berner**, printed a single broadsheet showing off its best types. That sheet — the *Egenolff-Berner specimen* — is the closest thing we have to a portrait of the romans cut by **Claude Garamont** in the mid-1500s and the italics of his contemporary **Robert Granjon**. It is also, four and a half centuries later, the source document for EB Garamond. The “EB” is a quiet act of citation: *Egenolff-Berner*.

The revival is the work of **Georg Duffner**, a librarian who taught himself type design to make the Garamond he wished existed, later joined by the type designer **Octavio Pardo**, who drew the bold weights and helped complete the italics. They worked the way a scholar works — straight from the 1592 specimen and from surviving Garamont punches — which is why EB Garamond is widely considered the most faithful free Garamond available anywhere.

What the faithfulness buys you is the genuine article: the warm, slightly irregular texture of Renaissance type, the slanted stress of the broad-nib pen, the small x-height and generous ascenders that make a page of it feel like a well-printed book. It carries old-style figures, small caps, ligatures and a true Granjon italic — the full apparatus of fine bookwork, given away for nothing.

In the arc of type history it sits at the centre of gravity. The **old-style** serif is the form everything reacted against — the transitional faces sharpened it, the Didones exaggerated it, the sans-serifs stripped its serifs away. EB Garamond is the baseline against which the rest of this collection measures itself.

Most corporate typefaces are quiet infrastructure. **IBM Plex** is unusual for having a thesis. When IBM set out to replace its decades-old reliance on licensed Helvetica, it asked its design team, led by **Mike Abbink**, and the Dutch foundry **Bold Monday** for something that could carry the company’s identity worldwide — and that could *say something* about IBM itself.

The idea they settled on was the relationship between humans and machines, a theme that has run through IBM’s history since the turn of the twentieth century. You can read it directly in the letterforms. Plex pulls in opposite directions on purpose: the rational, engineered skeleton of a grotesque, softened by humanist curves and finishing strokes drawn from old typewriter faces and the IBM logotype. The balance of engineering against feeling is, quite literally, the design.

The monospaced cut is where that tension is most fun to watch. A monospace exists to serve machines — every character locked to the same width so columns of code line up — and yet Plex Mono refuses to be cold about it. The two-storey **a** keeps a soft, nearly handwritten curve. The zero is unmistakably disambiguated. And the italic is not a lazy slant but a genuinely cursive design, the detail reviewers tend to single out as the family’s most charming.

Released open-source in 2017 and free to everyone, Plex has since been adopted far beyond IBM, beloved especially by developers who get to read its mono at arm’s length all day. In 2024 the Cooper Hewitt, Smithsonian Design Museum, accepted IBM Plex Sans and Mono into its permanent collection — a rare honour for a corporate typeface, and a fitting one for a face that always wanted to be about more than business.

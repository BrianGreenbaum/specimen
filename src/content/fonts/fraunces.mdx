In the summer of 2018, Google Fonts asked **Phaedra Charles** and **Flavia Zimbardi** to design a display typeface. Scrolling through the library, they noticed an odd gap. There was no good open-source home for the warm, slightly goofy serifs they loved — the lineage of Cooper Black, Windsor and Souvenir. In the studio they had always called these *wonky* fonts. The name stuck, and so did the brief.

The wonky style is not an accident of history; it is a deliberate one. It traces back to the Arts and Crafts movement, when designers like William Morris and the Roycroft Guild reached for a romanticised, hand-made *Old Style* as an antidote to industrial modernity. Those letters were never trying to be historically accurate. They were playful, a little overcooked, full of personality. Fraunces sets out to honour exactly that spirit rather than to revive any single face.

What makes it remarkable is how much of that personality is put under your control. Fraunces is a four-axis variable font. **Weight** runs thin to black. **Optical Size** stretches from a sober 9-point text cut to a flamboyant 144-point display, ramping up contrast and dropping the x-height as it grows. **Softness** controls the inkiness of the joints, from crisp to wet. And **WONK** — the showpiece — toggles the misbehaving alternates: the leaning *h, n, m* and the flagged ball terminals of the italic. With three optical sizes, three softness levels and a full range of weights, the family ships with more than a hundred named instances.

Because it is free and open, Fraunces has travelled far beyond what a brief could predict. Zimbardi, who is Brazilian, has watched it become a staple back home — turning up in branding, on packaging, even in a national television campaign — precisely because a beautiful, characterful serif made by a Brazilian and given away for nothing is a small act of generosity that designers repay by using it everywhere.

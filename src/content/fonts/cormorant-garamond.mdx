Most open-source revivals are made by foundries. Cormorant was made in the gaps. **Christian Thalmann** is a Swiss astronomer and physicist who draws type as a hobby, in the slivers of time left over from research and parenthood, under the name *Catharsis Fonts*. In 2015 he set out to make the Garamond he wanted but could not find — and, remarkably, did the whole thing himself: conceived, drawn, spaced, kerned, interpolated and produced by one person.

He was drawn to **Claude Garamond**, the sixteenth-century French punchcutter whose old-style romans have shaped Western typography ever since. But Thalmann had no interest in another faithful revival. Plenty of those already existed for reading at book sizes, where Garamond is exquisite. His complaint was the opposite end: blown up to headline size, traditional Garamond cuts turn clumpy and inelegant, their proportions tuned for a world of small metal type and spreading ink.

Cormorant is the answer to that gap. The goal, as he put it, was to distil the aesthetic essence of Garamond, free it from the constraints of metal, and let it bloom at high resolution. So the serifs are filed to razor points, the counters squeezed scandalously small, the contrast pushed high and the accents drawn flamboyantly tall — every move a luxury that only becomes possible at display size on a sharp screen or a fine press. He drew almost every glyph from scratch, checking himself against the broad impression of Garamond rather than copying any single face, and credits the TypeDrawers and Typophile forums for teaching him the craft as he went.

Released under the Open Font License, the family sprawled into dozens of files and styles, with the *Garamond* cut tuned for a touch more readability. Free, beautiful and faintly aristocratic, Cormorant Garamond found its natural habitat almost immediately — in branding, in editorial titling, and above all across the wedding-stationery world, where its graceful, high-contrast letters have become something close to a universal language for the occasion.

Two thousand years ago, a Roman signwriter climbed a scaffold with a flat brush and laid out the letters that still govern our capitals — wide, balanced, finished with a confident flick that the chisel turned into a serif. The grandest survivor is the inscription on **Trajan's Column**, but the form is everywhere in the imperial city, including the **Theatre of Marcellus**, whose name this typeface borrows.

Marcellus, drawn by **Brian J. Bonislawsky** for the Astigmatic foundry, is a modern revival of that lapidary capital. It keeps the monumental proportions, the gently **flared serifs**, and the long, performing tails of the **Q** and **R**, but draws them with a quiet, even refinement suited to the screen and the printed page. It is not a workhorse text face; it is a face for a title, a name, a single line that wants to feel as though it has always existed.

Set large and spaced generously, it lends the calm authority of carved stone to anything it touches — a museum wall, a wine label, the opening line of a book. In the timeline it sits at the very beginning, a reminder that the shape of our alphabet was settled, with extraordinary finality, before the printing press was even imagined.

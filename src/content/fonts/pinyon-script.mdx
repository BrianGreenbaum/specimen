The pointed pen reached its summit not in calligraphy's usual world of manuscripts and monks, but in the counting houses and engraving workshops of eighteenth-century England. **English roundhand** — later codified and spread across America by **Platt Rogers Spencer** as the hand that bears his name — was the script of commerce and ceremony: the hand that addressed invitations, signed bonds and letters of credit, inscribed diplomas and certificates, and gave luxury goods their names. It was a democratic elegance, teachable with practice and a quill, and it shaped the look of formality for two hundred years.

The key to its character is the **pointed pen** — a split nib of steel or quill that opens under pressure on the downstroke, leaving a swelling broad mark, then closes to a single hair on the upstroke. The result is a rhythm of extreme contrast: thick and thin alternating in every loop, every oval, every connecting stroke. Unlike the broad-nib scripts of the Renaissance, where the angle of the pen determined the stress, here the scribe controls every transition by touch. The copperplate engravers who reproduced the hand in metal only heightened this: an engraver's burin follows a path rather than a direction, allowing hairlines of barely measurable width beside stems four times as heavy.

**Nicole Fally** drew Pinyon Script in 2011 as a revival of this tradition — a digitisation of formal copperplate that captures the characteristic features without sacrificing the warmth that makes a handwritten letter feel personal. She preserved the sweeping **ascender loops**, the ornate **swash capitals** that open each word like a flourish, and the delicate **entry and exit strokes** that link letter to letter in an unbroken ribbon. Released via Google Fonts under the SIL Open Font License, it brought this register of formal script within reach of designers who could not afford or source historical revivals from boutique foundries.

The discipline Pinyon Script demands is the same discipline the original scripts demanded: **only at display size, only for short phrases, only set in mixed case.** The connecting strokes that make it beautiful at eighty points become an illegible tangle at twelve; the swash capitals that signal ceremony become noise if the whole phrase is uppercased. Pair it with a roman companion — **EB Garamond** or **Spectral** — for any supporting text. Give it room, give it size, and it does what no serif can: it makes a name look as though someone took the trouble to write it by hand.

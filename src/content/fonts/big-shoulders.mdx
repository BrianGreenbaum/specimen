Carl Sandburg called Chicago the *“City of the Big Shoulders”* — hog butcher, tool maker, stacker of wheat, a place that did the heavy work and stood up straight while doing it. A century later that line became a typeface. **Big Shoulders** was drawn by **Patric King** at OH no Type Co. as part of a civic identity for the City of Chicago, the rare retail-quality family commissioned to wear a city’s name.

The brief is written into every letter. The forms are tall, narrow and condensed, curves squared off against the vertical so that even an O or a G reads as upright and structural — letters built like the steel frames that made the skyscraper a Chicago invention. The contrast is low and the x-height high, the priorities of lettering meant to be read across a street or a stockyard: loud, sturdy, legible at speed.

What turns it from a single civic logo into a working family is its range. Big Shoulders is variable across weight and optical size, and it spans an enormous gamut — from a whisper-thin hairline up to a slab-solid black, in both a display cut for headlines and a text cut tuned for smaller sizes. Drag the weight from one extreme to the other and you watch the same industrial skeleton go from delicate to immovable without ever losing its broad-shouldered stance.

Open source and free, it offers something the web is short on: a display face with a sense of place. Most type tries to be from nowhere in particular. Big Shoulders is unmistakably from somewhere — and proud of the work that city is named for.

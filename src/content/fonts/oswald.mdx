When the 19th century learned to shout, it reached for the **condensed gothic**: tall, narrow, serif-less capitals that could pack a whole proclamation onto a single broadside and still be legible from across the street. The American foundries called the style **Alternate Gothic**, and it became the native voice of the poster, the playbill, and the newspaper headline.

Oswald, drawn by **Vernon Adams** and developed for Google Fonts (later extended by Kalapi Gajjar and Cyreal), is that tradition rebuilt for the screen. It takes the classic condensed gothic skeleton — the narrow widths, the tall x-height, the squared-off **M** and spurred **G** — and re-proportions it pixel by pixel for clean rendering at every size. The variable file runs from a quiet Extra Light to a hard-hitting Bold.

It is, unapologetically, a headline face. Set it large and tight and it does exactly what the poster gothic was invented to do: grab the eye, hold the line, and make a few words feel like an event. In the timeline it stands among the great grotesques — the moment the serif fell away and type learned, for the first time, to raise its voice.

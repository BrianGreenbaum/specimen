Playfair Display is set in the eighteenth century by intent. The Danish designer **Claus Eggers Sørensen** released it in 2011, but its imagination belongs to the Enlightenment — the moment when better paper, smoother ink and sharper steel let printers cut letters of high contrast and fine hairlines, increasingly detached from the marks a pen could actually make. Sørensen was not reviving any one face. He was channelling a period: the world of **John Baskerville** and the Scotch Romans that followed him.

That heritage shows in every stroke. The contrast between thick and thin is dramatic, almost theatrical. The stress is vertical and rational. Strokes resolve into delicate round ball terminals, a flourish borrowed from the engraver’s burin and the pointed pen. The serifs are thin and finely bracketed. All of it is tuned for large sizes — for the title, the masthead, the fashion headline — where those fragile hairlines have room to thrive.

Use it the wrong way and it tells you. At small sizes the contrast that makes it beautiful starts to fight legibility; the hairlines thin to nothing and the page sparkles uncomfortably. Sørensen designed it knowing this, pairing it with workhorse serifs like Georgia for the body and reserving Playfair for the moments that deserve a flourish.

The face has kept growing. A major revision arrived in 2017, and in 2023 it was effectively succeeded by the broader **Playfair** family — a three-axis variable design with width, weight and a true optical-size axis that finally adds text-size cuts. But the original *Display* remains one of the most recognisable serifs on the web, the go-to whenever a page needs to feel a little more expensive than it is.

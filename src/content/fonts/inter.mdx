Inter began, fittingly, as an act of self-defence against bad type on screen. In 2016 the Swedish designer **Rasmus Andersson** — then working on interfaces at Figma — found himself wanting a sans-serif tuned for exactly the situation he spent his days in: small text, rendered on a screen, read in a hurry. He started drawing one in the evenings and called it *Interface*. The name was later trimmed to **Inter**, but the brief never changed.

Everything about the design answers to legibility at small sizes. The x-height is tall, so each letter fills more of its space and holds its colour in a paragraph. The apertures — the openings in letters like **c**, **e** and **s** — are kept wide, because narrow openings are the first thing to clog up when a curve meets a coarse pixel grid. Spacing is even and slightly generous. None of it is showy. That restraint is the point.

What turned a personal project into one of the most-used typefaces of the decade was the way Andersson built and shared it. Inter is free and open source, developed in the open on GitHub and refreshed continually rather than frozen at a 1.0. It grew a vast OpenType repertoire along the way: tabular and slashed-zero figures for data, a single-storey **a**, case-sensitive punctuation, and dozens of character variants. The variable version folds the whole family into one file with a weight axis and an optical-size axis that subtly adjusts the design as it grows.

The result is everywhere. Inter sets the interfaces of GitHub, Mozilla and countless products, apps and design systems — so thoroughly that it has become a kind of default voice for the modern software era. Its great trick is that you rarely notice it, which is the highest compliment you can pay a typeface built for reading.

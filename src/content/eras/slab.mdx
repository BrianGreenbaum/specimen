For three hundred years, type had one job: the book. The Industrial Revolution gave it a dozen new ones. Posters, handbills, packaging and advertisements needed letters that could be set enormous and black, and read at a glance from across a busy street. The delicate book faces were hopeless for it, so the 19th century invented the **slab serif** — a sturdy letter with heavy, rectangular **serifs** bolted on, low in **contrast** and built to survive at any size.

The Victorians, swept up in the era’s Egyptomania, nicknamed the loudest of them *Egyptians*. The most refined branch, **Clarendon** — cut by **Robert Besley** in 1845 — softened the slabs with gentle brackets, making them strong without being brutal. This was the first typography designed for selling rather than reading, and its DNA runs straight through every bold headline and display face that followed.

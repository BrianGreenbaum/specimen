For three hundred years, type had essentially one job: the book. The Industrial Revolution handed it a dozen new ones at once. Railways, factories and mass markets filled the streets with posters, handbills, packaging and advertisements — and all of them needed letters that could be set enormous and black, and read at a glance by someone hurrying past. The delicate book faces, and the flickering hairlines of the Didone, were hopeless for it.

So the nineteenth century built a new kind of letter from scratch. The **slab serif** kept the serif but thickened it into a heavy rectangular block, flattened the **contrast** until the strokes were nearly even, and piled on weight until the whole letter read as a solid mass of ink. It was loud, blunt and entirely unliterary — and it was exactly what the new economy demanded.

<PullQuote cite="Besley · Clarendon, revived" family="Besley Variable">
  Built to be read from a moving carriage.
</PullQuote>

The Victorians, in the grip of the era’s **Egyptomania** after Napoleon’s campaigns on the Nile, nicknamed the loudest of these faces *Egyptians* — a name with nothing to do with Egypt and everything to do with fashion. They came in fat versions and condensed versions, shadowed and outlined and three-dimensional, anything to win a few more inches of attention on a crowded wall.

<Divider label="V" />

<Spread tone="paper-2">
The most refined branch was **Clarendon**, cut by **Robert Besley** in 1845. By joining its heavy serifs to the stems with a soft curved **bracket**, Clarendon found a sweet spot — strong enough to anchor a headline, even enough to read in a caption. It became the workhorse of the genre, and you still meet its descendants every day, on dictionary entries, emergency signage and the typography of the American West.
</Spread>

This was, in a real sense, the first typography designed for **selling** rather than reading — the birth of advertising type — and its DNA runs straight through every bold headline since.

But the slab had only removed the hairline. The truly radical move — taking the serif off altogether — was already sitting, ignored, in a London type catalogue from 1816. The nineteenth century had printed it and walked past it. It would take the modern world a century to understand what it had.

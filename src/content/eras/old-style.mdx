When printing arrived in Europe in the 1450s, the first types imitated the dense, thorny gothic handwriting of the north — Gutenberg’s Bible is set in it. But in Italy, humanist scholars rummaging through monastic libraries were reading the classics in a rounder, clearer, altogether sunnier hand. They believed it was the script of ancient Rome. It was actually Carolingian, four centuries old — but the mistake was a gift, because it sent the Renaissance reaching for a letter as open and rational as its ideals.

In Venice, printers like **Nicolas Jenson** cut type to match that humanist hand, and for the first time the **lowercase** met the Roman capital on equal terms. The **old-style** serif was born — and with it, very nearly the whole alphabet you are reading now.

<PullQuote cite="EB Garamond, after Claude Garamont" family="EB Garamond Variable">
  The hand returns to the letter, and the letter learns to breathe.
</PullQuote>

Its character comes straight from the broad-nib pen the scribes used. Hold such a pen at a slant and the thinnest part of every round letter falls on a diagonal — the **stress** leans. The **contrast** between thick and thin stays gentle, the **serifs** are bracketed, flowing into the stem on a soft curve, and the whole page reads warm and even, almost like breathing. It is the most comfortable text colour our alphabet has ever produced; we have spent five hundred years failing to improve on it for the reading of long books.

<Divider label="II" />

<Spread tone="tint">
Venice gave the era a second invention, too. To fit cheap, portable editions of the classics, the publisher **Aldus Manutius** had **Francesco Griffo** cut a slanted, cursive type based on the chancery hand — the first **italic**. It began as a way to save space; it became the universal mark of emphasis and the voice of the aside. Three of the styles in your font menu — roman, italic, and the capital itself — were essentially settled in a single Venetian generation.
</Spread>

Then **Claude Garamont** in Paris brought it all to its peak. His romans were so exquisitely judged that *Garamond* became a synonym for the classic book, and his decision to sell type as an independent **foundry** — rather than cutting it for one printer’s exclusive use — quietly split the design of letters from their use, and invented the type business as we know it.

For two and a half centuries this warm, humane letter was simply what type *was*. It would take the Enlightenment — and a writing master in Birmingham with a grudge against soft edges — to start drawing the letter with a ruler instead of a pen. That is where we go next.

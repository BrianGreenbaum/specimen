When printing arrived in the 1450s, the first types imitated the dense gothic handwriting of northern Europe. But in Italy, humanist scholars were reading classical texts in a rounder, clearer hand — and printers like **Nicolas Jenson** in Venice cut type to match it. For the first time, the lowercase letter and the capital met on equal terms, and the **old-style** serif was born.

Its character comes straight from the broad-nib pen that the scribes used. Hold such a pen at a slant and the thinnest part of every round letter falls on a diagonal — the **stress** leans. The **contrast** between thick and thin stays gentle, the **serifs** are bracketed, and the whole page reads warm and even. **Claude Garamont** perfected the form in the 1500s so thoroughly that "a Garamond" still means *the* classic book face, four centuries on.

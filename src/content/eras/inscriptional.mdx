The story of our alphabet does not begin in a print shop. It begins on the side of a building. The capital letters we still use were brought to their highest resolution by the carvers of Imperial Rome, whose monumental inscriptions set proportions so complete that every typographer since has been, in some sense, copying them.

The technique left its fingerprints. Letters were first painted onto the stone with a flat brush, which naturally swelled and thinned the strokes and flicked out a small serif at each end; the chisel then followed the paint. That is why these forms have **contrast** and flared serifs at all — not as decoration, but as the honest trace of the tools. There was no lowercase and no italic. This was the alphabet as architecture: upright, formal, and built to last two thousand years.

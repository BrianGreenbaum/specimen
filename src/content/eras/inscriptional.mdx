The story of our alphabet does not begin in a print shop. It begins on the side of a building. The capital letters we still use were brought to their highest resolution not by scribes but by the carvers of Imperial Rome, whose monumental inscriptions set proportions so complete that every typographer since has been, in some quiet sense, copying them.

Stand at the foot of **Trajan's Column** in Rome and look up at the dedication panel carved around 113 AD. The letters are barely a hand high, yet they read cleanly from the pavement — wide, balanced, serene. For two millennia, designers have measured their own work against this single inscription. When you reach for the word *classic*, this is, almost literally, what you are reaching for.

<PullQuote cite="Father Edward Catich, The Origin of the Serif" family="Cinzel Variable">
  The serif was born of the brush, not the chisel.
</PullQuote>

The technique left its fingerprints all over the form. Letters were first painted onto the stone with a flat, square-cut brush, which naturally swelled and thinned the strokes and flicked out a small **serif** at each end; only then did the chisel follow the paint. That is why these forms have **contrast** and flared serifs at all — not as decoration, but as the honest trace of the tools. The diagonal thinning of the round **O**, the flick at the foot of the **R**: each is a brushstroke, frozen in marble.

<Spread tone="dark">
There was no lowercase. There was no italic. There were no spaces between words, no question marks, no numerals as we know them — just twenty-odd **capitals**, cut in rows and read aloud. This was the alphabet as **architecture**: upright, formal, monumental, and built, quite deliberately, to outlast the people who made it.

What the Romans could not have known is how completely they were setting the defaults. The proportions they tuned by eye — the narrow **E**, the wide **O**, the generous **M** — became the skeleton beneath the printed capital a thousand years before anyone dreamed of movable type.
</Spread>

When printers finally came, in the 1450s, they would spend decades trying to give the lowercase the same dignity these capitals already had. That is the next chapter. But the capital arrived first, arrived complete, and has barely changed since. Everything that follows in this history is, in one way or another, a negotiation with Rome.

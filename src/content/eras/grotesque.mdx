Around 1816, a strange new letter appeared in a type catalogue: a roman with its **serifs** simply removed. It looked so odd to eyes raised on serifs that the style was called *grotesque* — and, in Britain, *gothic*. For decades it was treated as a novelty, fit only for the odd line of emphasis. Then the industrial poster discovered it, and the sans-serif began its long climb to dominance.

These early sans faces were drawn by eye, not by system. They keep a faint **contrast** and a slightly irregular, hand-felt quality — sturdy, neutral and a touch awkward in the best way. **Akzidenz-Grotesk**, cut in Berlin in 1898, became the template the whole genre would later refine. It is the rough, confident ancestor of every clean sans-serif that followed — including the neo-grotesques and screen faces at the end of this story.

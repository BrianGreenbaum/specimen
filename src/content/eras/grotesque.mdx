Around 1816, a strange new letter appeared, almost as an afterthought, in a London type catalogue: a row of capitals with the **serifs** simply removed. To eyes raised on three centuries of serifs it looked crude, even ugly — and the trade gave it a telling name. In Germany it was *grotesk*; in Britain, *gothic*. Both words meant roughly the same thing: *barbarous*. Nobody imagined they were looking at the future.

For decades the sans-serif lived as a novelty, useful only for a stray line of emphasis on a poster. But it had one quality the industrial world increasingly prized — it was *plain*. No history, no ornament, no calligraphic baggage; just the bare bones of the letter. Slowly, through tickets and timetables and shopfronts, the plain letter spread.

<PullQuote cite="Space Grotesk" family="Space Grotesk Variable">
  Strip away the serif, and what remains is the modern world.
</PullQuote>

These early grotesques were still drawn by eye, not by system. Look closely and you find a faint **contrast**, a slight unevenness, a hand-felt quality — sturdy, neutral and just a touch awkward, in the best way. **Akzidenz-Grotesk**, gathered together in Berlin in 1898, distilled all of it into one workmanlike face and, in doing so, wrote the template the entire twentieth century would refine.

<Divider label="VI" />

<Spread tone="dark">
That refinement reached its peak in 1957, in a small Swiss town, when **Max Miedinger** and **Eduard Hoffmann** sanded Akzidenz-Grotesk into something flawlessly even and called it **Helvetica** — “the Swiss.” Within a generation it was everywhere: the typeface of airlines, governments, the New York subway and a thousand logos. The barbarous letter of 1816 had become the most trusted voice of modern life, precisely because it seemed to have no voice at all.
</Spread>

The grotesque had won by subtraction — by taking things away. But a second group of modernists, working at the very same time, distrusted the hand that had drawn even these plain letters. They wanted to throw out the eye altogether and rebuild the alphabet from pure geometry. Their letter would be designed, not drawn — and it is where we go next.

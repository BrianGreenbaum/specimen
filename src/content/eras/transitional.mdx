By the mid-1700s, the Enlightenment’s appetite for precision reached the printed page. In Birmingham, **John Baskerville** — a writing master turned printer — smoothed his paper, blackened his ink and sharpened his presses so that finer letters would survive the impression. His type marked a turn: away from the calligraphic old-style and toward something drawn with reason and a ruler.

It is called **transitional** because it sits exactly between two worlds. The **stress** straightens toward vertical, the **contrast** rises, the **serifs** grow crisper — yet the letters keep enough warmth to read comfortably at length. Baskerville’s contemporaries thought it almost too sharp on the eye; today it reads as calm, rational and quietly authoritative. It is the hinge on which the serif swings from the pen toward the machine.

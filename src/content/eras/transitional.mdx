By the middle of the eighteenth century, the Enlightenment’s appetite for precision had reached the printed page. The warm, pen-drawn old-style had served the book for nearly three hundred years; now a generation raised on reason and measurement wanted letters that looked *designed* rather than written — and the technology had finally caught up enough to let them have it.

In Birmingham, **John Baskerville** — a writing master and japanner turned printer — set out to make the most perfect book ever printed. He smoothed and hot-pressed his paper, blackened his ink until it gleamed, and rebuilt his presses so the finest strokes would survive the impression. The type he produced in the 1750s marked a clear turn: away from the calligraphic hand, toward something drawn with a ruler and an idea.

<PullQuote cite="Libre Baskerville, after John Baskerville" family="Libre Baskerville">
  Drawn with a ruler, not a pen.
</PullQuote>

It is called **transitional** because it stands exactly between two worlds. The **stress** straightens toward vertical, the **contrast** rises, the **serifs** grow crisper and finer — yet the letters keep just enough warmth and bracketing to read comfortably across a whole book. It is the hinge on which the serif swings from the pen toward the machine.

<Spread tone="paper-2">
Baskerville’s contemporaries did not all thank him. Rivals complained that his glittering, high-contrast letters were too sharp — that they would, quite literally, damage the eyes of English readers. The objection was really about fashion and commerce, as **Benjamin Franklin** delighted in proving. Today the very qualities they feared read as calm, rational and quietly authoritative. Baskerville is the sound of an argument being made well.
</Spread>

The contemporary face beside Baskerville on this page, **Newsreader**, shows how alive this tradition still is: a screen-native text serif, built by Production Type, that keeps the upright stress and reading colour of the eighteenth century while adding a modern optical-size axis. The transitional temper — literate, measured, trustworthy — never went out of print.

Reason

But reason, pushed hard enough, becomes spectacle. Within a single generation, printers in Parma and Paris would take Baskerville’s rising contrast and run with it — until the thick strokes turned to slabs and the thin ones nearly vanished. The serif was about to stop being about reading at all.

Every era before this one designed type for ink on paper. Ours designs it for **light on a grid**. It is the most fundamental change of surface since the chisel gave way to the printing press — and, like that earlier shift, it sent designers back to first principles to ask what a letter even is when its medium changes underneath it.

The first answer was brutal arithmetic. Early screens could spare only a handful of glowing squares per character, so the alphabet was rebuilt, pixel by pixel, inside a grid — **Susan Kare** drawing the first Macintosh fonts on graph paper, one black square at a time. There was no room for hairlines, brackets or grace; there was barely room for the letter.

<PullQuote cite="Inter, by Rasmus Andersson" family="Inter Variable">
  A face you read all day without ever noticing it.
</PullQuote>

As screens sharpened, the craft matured into a discipline. To survive rendering, designers raised the **x-height** so more of each letter lands in the readable band, opened the **apertures** so curves would not clog, evened the spacing and tested everything on the grid first. The result is a new kind of neutrality — faces like **Inter** and **Source Sans** that disappear into the interface, asking nothing of you but to be read, all day, without fatigue or notice.

<Divider label="VIII" />

<Spread tone="dark">
The deepest revolution, though, is not any single typeface — it is a *file*. The **variable font** packs an entire family — every weight, width and **optical size** — into one document whose axes you can set live, even animate, in the browser. For five centuries a typeface was a fixed thing, cut once in metal. Now it bends in real time to the screen in front of you. Every tester, waterfall and morphing letter in this library runs on that single idea.
</Spread>

And so the long journey closes a circle. Five hundred years after **Garamont** cut his punches one immovable size at a time, the letter has finally come unstuck — fluid, responsive, free. Drag the letter at the start of this history back and forth and you are not just scrubbing a timeline; you are doing the thing the whole story was secretly about. From Roman stone to the variable font, the alphabet has been waiting, all along, to move.

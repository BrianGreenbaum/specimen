Every era before this one designed type for ink on paper. Ours designs it for light on a grid. When letters had to survive being rendered out of a handful of pixels, type designers went back to fundamentals: raise the **x-height** so more of each letter lands in the readable band, open the **apertures** so curves don’t clog, even the spacing, and test everything on screen first. The result is a new kind of neutrality — faces like **Inter** that you read all day without ever noticing.

The deeper revolution is technical. The **variable font** packs an entire family — every weight, width and **optical size** — into a single file whose axes you can set live in the browser, even animate. It is the idea that runs through this whole collection: type as something fluid and responsive rather than fixed in metal. Five hundred years after Garamont cut his punches one immovable size at a time, the letter has finally come unstuck — free to flex, in real time, to fit the screen in front of you.

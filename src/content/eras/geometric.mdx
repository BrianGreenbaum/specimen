In the 1920s, a generation of modernists looked at the alphabet and decided it was overdue for demolition. The hand that had shaped every letter since Rome was, to them, a relic of a dead world. The future would not be *written* — it would be **constructed**, drawn on a drafting table from the elementary shapes of geometry, as rationally and as honestly as a steel-and-glass building.

At the Bauhaus and in the circle around *Die Neue Typographie*, the new letter took shape: an **o** that is very nearly a perfect circle, an **a** stripped to a single storey, strokes of dead-even **monoline** weight with every trace of the eye’s irregularity engineered out. Where the grotesque had been plain by accident of craft, the geometric sans was plain on purpose, by design.

<PullQuote cite="Jost, after Paul Renner" family="Jost Variable">
  Not written. Constructed.
</PullQuote>

The masterpiece was **Futura**, drawn by **Paul Renner** between 1924 and 1927. Crisp, optimistic and unmistakably *modern*, it became the visual signature of the century — on book jackets and brand marks, in Stanley Kubrick’s films, and finally on the commemorative plaque Apollo 11 left on the Moon. Few letters have travelled further from the chisel.

<Divider label="VII" />

<Spread tone="tint">
The movement was as political as it was formal. Renner’s anti-Nazi stand cost him his post in 1933; the Bauhaus itself was forced to close. Yet the regimes that denounced “degenerate” modern design kept reaching for its clean, efficient letters, and after the war the geometric sans became the global language of confidence and progress — the look of the airline, the bank, the world’s fair.
</Spread>

A century on, that dream simply refuses to age. The warm, rounded **Outfit** beside Jost on this page shows the geometric idea remade for today’s screens and brands — the same circle and line, smiling a little more easily.

The geometric sans is where the alphabet travels furthest from the hand. But one frontier remained that none of these designers could have imagined — a surface made not of paper but of light, divided into a grid of glowing squares. The letter was about to be reinvented one more time, for the screen.

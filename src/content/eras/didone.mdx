By the 1790s, paper, ink and steel had all improved enough to let printers do something previously impossible: cut letters of breathtaking **contrast**. **Giambattista Bodoni** in Parma and **Firmin Didot** in Paris pushed the transitional serif to its logical extreme — massive vertical stems dropping to **hairlines** so fine they look engraved, finished with flat, **unbracketed serifs** that meet the stem at a perfect right angle.

The result, the **Didone** or "modern" serif, traded warmth for drama. It is harder to read in long passages — those hairlines flicker and break up — but at large sizes it is pure glamour. That is exactly why, two centuries later, it still rules the fashion masthead: from *Vogue* to *Harper’s Bazaar*, the look of luxury is a Didone. It is the moment the rational serif stopped being about reading and became about *style*.
